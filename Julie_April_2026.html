What We Measured
GYMGEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped. A direct measure of explosive leg power.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time (eccentric + concentric + flight). Rewards both height and speed of execution. GymGeek uses the ForceDecks total-contraction-time method; values are typically lower than flight-time-only protocols.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump, divided by bodyweight. Shows how explosively force is generated per kilogram.
Leg Asymmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force recorded during a maximal isometric pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength. Note: IMTP scores are sensitive to testing setup and technique; treat as a strong directional indicator rather than a precise ranking.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly force is produced in the first 200 milliseconds of the isometric pull. Higher values indicate faster, more explosive neuromuscular function.
Postural Sway
Sway Velocity. The average speed of the body's centre of pressure whilst standing still, in mm/s. Lower values indicate better postural stability and balance control.

Julie, here's how you got on
What's working well. The standout results from this session are your lower body strength and your leg symmetry. Your maximum force score on the isometric pull sits at the 93rd percentile compared to active women your age, placing you firmly in the top category. That is a genuinely strong result, and while IMTP scores are sensitive to setup and technique so the precise rank should be read as directional, the raw number (2.00 times your bodyweight) is clearly above the typical range for this group. Your rate of force development (how quickly that strength fires in the first 200 milliseconds) is equally impressive at the 94th percentile: your nervous system is already very capable of switching on quickly during the pull. Postural stability is also well above average at the 81st percentile, and your leg symmetry is exceptional at 2.49% difference between sides, sitting in the top 1% of all women tested.
Where there's room to improve. The jump-based metrics tell a very different story. Your jump height (10.61 cm), reactive strength (0.08), and explosive power (4.67 W/kg) all sit at the bottom of the range compared to active women in their 50s. This is a significant gap between your isometric strength and your dynamic, ballistic output: the legs clearly have force capacity, but they are not currently expressing it quickly in a free movement like a jump. This kind of split is not uncommon and is not necessarily a concern on its own, but it is worth noting alongside the left-side leg pain you mentioned. Reduced jump output can sometimes reflect caution or altered movement strategy around a painful area, even when asymmetry data looks balanced. It would be worth having the left-side pain assessed by a professional if you have not already done so.
The bigger picture. What this session tells us is that your body is capable of producing genuinely impressive force: the isometric pull and rate of force scores are well above what most active women your age produce, and your symmetry and stability scores suggest the foundations are in a good place. The low scores on jump height, reactive strength, and explosive power are not a reflection of overall fitness but of a specific quality: the ability to produce force fast in a free, dynamic movement. That gap between what your legs can grind out slowly and what they express quickly is the central finding from this session. It is also worth getting the left-side pain properly assessed if you have not already done so, as that context matters when interpreting the jump-based results.
