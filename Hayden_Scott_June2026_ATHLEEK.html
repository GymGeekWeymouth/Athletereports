What We Measured
ATHLEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped from a countermovement start. One of the most well-normed metrics in the report. Affected by technique, ankle mobility and body proportions.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time. Rewards both how high and how quickly you jump: a measure of spring and explosiveness. Sensitive to technique and responsive to plyometric and reactive training.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump per kilogram of bodyweight. Can be elevated by a deep countermovement; best interpreted alongside jump height and RSI together rather than in isolation.
Leg Symmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced. A well-normed and practically meaningful metric: one of the most reliable results in this report.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force produced during a maximal pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength with good literature support. Responds strongly to structured loading; pushing it higher relative to bodyweight is a key development lever.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly force rises in the first 200 milliseconds of a maximal pull. Captures neuromuscular speed and explosiveness. Presented as a descriptive category rather than a ranked band in this report because it is highly sensitive to testing setup, sampling rate, and onset detection: it is most useful as a longitudinal tracking metric over time.
Postural Stability
Sway Velocity. Average speed of the body's centre of pressure while standing still. Lower is better. Presented as a descriptive category: most useful as a longitudinal baseline and for fatigue or return-to-play monitoring.

Hayden, here's how you got on
A strong start

First things first: this is a baseline, a clean reference point to measure progress against as you step up to step 4. There is a technique element to the testing, particularly the countermovement jump, so if this is one of your early sessions on the plates a score or two may sit slightly under your true capability and tighten up at retest. Either way, what follows is an honest snapshot of where you are right now and, more usefully, where the biggest gains are.

The headline is your power. Your explosive power is excellent: at 54.0 W/kg you sit at the very top of the active-male range, and that reading competes even with full-time Premier League and EFL professionals. Importantly it is backed up by a solid 38 cm jump, which tells us it is a real quality rather than a quirk of technique. For a centre midfielder that explosiveness is gold: the sharp first steps to win a second ball, the closing speed to press, the spring to compete in the air. It is exactly the sort of athletic base that travels well as you step up a level.

Around that, you are a well-rounded athlete. Your jump height comes in slightly above the active-male average, with maximum strength and reactive strength sitting right around it, and your leg symmetry at 8.35% is within the balanced sub-10% range. There is no glaring weakness in this profile against everyday active men; you come out as a genuinely solid, powerful all-rounder with a clear standout quality. One small note on the power figure: a very deep countermovement can nudge a peak power reading upwards, so we will confirm the exact number at your next session with a standardised depth, but the underlying quality is not in doubt.

Where the next level is

Because you stack up so well against active peers, the most useful comparison is the professional benchmark, and that is where the development story lives. Two qualities stand out. The first is reactive spring: your raw power is excellent, but reactive strength (RSI 0.43) measures how fast you can express it, and that is where full-time pros pull clearest daylight. The second is relative strength, your force per kilogram of bodyweight; at 92.6 kg you carry a powerful frame, and lifting strength-to-weight is what makes the jumping, accelerating and physical duelling all sharper. Neither is a weakness; both are the natural next step for a player moving up the pyramid.

Your maximum force came out at 2.91 times bodyweight, a sound base, with rate of force development noted as descriptive only this time (it was taken from the cleanest of three pulls). The opportunity here is clear and motivating: you already own the power, so a structured block of strength and reactive work has a very direct route to translate it into the jumping, sprinting and spring qualities that define the level above.

Postural stability read slightly elevated, which is presented as descriptive only. It is best treated as a baseline to return to for fatigue and return-to-play monitoring rather than a cause for concern at this stage.
