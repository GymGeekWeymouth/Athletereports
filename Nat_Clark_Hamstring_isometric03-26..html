What the literature tells us

No tendinopathy-specific norms exist for this test configuration. There is currently no published normative dataset for hamstring isometric force in people with proximal or distal hamstring tendinopathy. The 10% asymmetry threshold used here comes from return-to-sport and loading-progression criteria in hamstring strain injury rehabilitation (Taberner & Cohen 2020), which is the closest available clinical benchmark.

The left limb looks healthy. At 2.65 N/kg, the unaffected leg matches a typical active female population. This is a useful internal reference point.

The right limb has a clear deficit. At 2.04 N/kg it sits 23% below the left and roughly 22% below the active population mean. The RFD and force decay data suggest the deficit is not just in how much force the right hamstring can produce, but in how quickly it ramps up and how long it can sustain it.

Nat, here is what the hamstring test shows
What's clear from this test. The right hamstring is carrying a meaningful deficit compared to the left. Peak force on the right is 11.91 kg versus 15.46 kg on the left: a 23% difference that is more than double the 10% threshold used clinically to determine when it is safe to progress hamstring loading in rehabilitation. Mean force and impulse tell the same story; the right hamstring produces and sustains less force across the entire contraction. Contraction duration is the one metric within the 10% threshold, at 7.7%, which suggests the right leg is trying to compensate by holding on longer, even though it is producing less force. That pattern is typical in a tendinopathic limb.
The explosive side is where the gap is largest. Rate of force development shows a 51% asymmetry: the right hamstring produces force at roughly half the speed of the left. This matters particularly for a runner, where fast hamstring activation in the late swing phase is a central demand of the gait cycle. The right also takes 210 milliseconds longer to reach peak force than the left. Alongside this, the force decay rate on the right is 2.6 times faster than the left: once the right hamstring peaks, it drops off quickly, suggesting the muscle-tendon unit fatigues or inhibits more rapidly on the affected side. These are not just strength deficits: they reflect altered neuromuscular drive to the tendinopathic limb, which is a well-documented response in tendinopathy even when the athlete feels broadly functional.
The left limb provides a good internal target. At 2.65 N/kg normalised peak force, the unaffected left hamstring sits right at the active female population mean, which means it is a credible and achievable rehab target for the right. Getting the right limb's peak force, RFD, and force decay back to within 10% of the left is the most specific and meaningful benchmark to track through rehabilitation. This test provides a clean baseline to retest against in 6 to 12 weeks as loading progresses.
A note on this test. No published normative data exists for hamstring isometric force in people with hamstring tendinopathy using this protocol configuration. The 10% asymmetry threshold cited here is drawn from hamstring strain injury rehabilitation literature (Taberner & Cohen 2020; Ripley et al. 2022) and is the closest available clinical reference point. These results are intended to support clinical decision-making alongside symptom response, clinical examination, and loading tolerance: not as a standalone return-to-sport criterion. Please share with the treating physiotherapist.
