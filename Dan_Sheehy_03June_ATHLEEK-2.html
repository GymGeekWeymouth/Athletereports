About the comparison data

Rather than chucking a precise percentile at you, we use banded classifications: Bottom 10%, Bottom Third, Average, Upper Third, Top 10%. That is more honest given how much normative datasets vary across labs, equipment, and populations. RFD and postural sway are presented as descriptive categories only as the norms are shakier. Worth flagging: all three of your IMTP attempts had slightly noisy baselines (b_SD 12.8–13.3 N), so attempt 1 was picked as the best of the three. A bit more stillness before each pull next time will sharpen those numbers up. As ever, all of this sits alongside your coach, your training history, and your goals.

What We Measured
ATHLEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high you got off the ground from a countermovement start. One of the most well-normed metrics we have. Sensitive to technique, how deep you dip, and hip mobility, all of which are trainable and will improve with practice.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time. Rewards both how high and how fast you jump: a proper measure of spring and explosiveness. Dan's is exceptional because he moved very quickly through the countermovement, which is exactly what this metric is picking up.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump per kilogram of bodyweight. Dan's 49.2 W/kg is exceptional. Can be nudged up by a deep countermovement, so best read alongside RSI and jump height rather than on its own.
Leg Symmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced. Dan's 6.2% is fine, no meaningful imbalance to flag.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force produced during a maximal pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength. One of the most directly trainable metrics in this profile and the one with the most room to move.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly force rises in the first 200 milliseconds of the pull. Presented as descriptive only because it is very sensitive to baseline noise, and all three of today's attempts had slightly elevated variation in the baseline window. Most useful as a tracking number once the test set-up is tightened up.
Postural Stability
Sway Velocity. Average speed of the body's centre of pressure while standing still. Lower is better. Dan's 4.5 mm/s is a very tidy score. Presented descriptive only: most useful as a longitudinal baseline and for flagging fatigue and readiness through a season.

Proper elite qualities in here, Dan

The headline from today is a good one. Your power and reactive strength numbers are exceptional, and that is not something we say lightly. At 49.2 W/kg, your peak power sits well above the average for active men under 40 and above what we typically see from Premier League and EFL professionals. Most people who come through this lab, whatever their sport or background, don't get close to that range. Your RSI of 0.69 backs it up from a different angle: top 10% of active men under 40, and upper third even against full-time professional footballers. Taken together, those two numbers paint a picture of a player with a serious fast-twitch engine. Explosive, spring-loaded, and naturally sharp in the kind of short-burst movement that a left winger lives off.

Worth being straight about one thing though. Peak power in the countermovement jump can be nudged upwards by a fast or deep dip before take-off, and that may be contributing here. That doesn't take anything away from the result, but it does mean we want to confirm it with a more consistent technique at your next session so we know exactly what we are dealing with. Either way, the directional conclusion holds: the fast-twitch qualities are very much there.

Where the work is

The flip side of the profile is maximum force. The IMTP came in at 2.67 times bodyweight, which is on the lower side against both the active male population and the professional benchmark. Here is what makes that interesting rather than concerning: you have elite-level power but a developing strength ceiling. That means the muscles are firing brilliantly at the loads they currently deal with, but there is a meaningful amount of additional output sitting there waiting once the force base grows. For a winger, maximum strength feeds directly into acceleration, holding off challenges, and being physically robust across a long season. It is also one of the most trainable things we measure: it responds reliably and well to structured gym work.

Jump height at 28.3 cm is in a similar position, and the two are connected. As maximum strength goes up, jump height tends to follow, particularly when you add some plyometric work on top. It is also worth mentioning that all three of the isometric pulls today had slightly noisy baselines, which can affect the quality of the force trace and the RFD reading. Nothing to do with effort or how hard Dan pulled: just a bit of movement during the standing phase beforehand. Standing a little stiller before each pull next time will sharpen those numbers up considerably.

Leg symmetry at 6.2% is in the well-balanced range, no concerns there. And the postural stability score of 4.5 mm/s is a very tidy number: excellent balance and postural control, which is a genuine positive and a useful marker to carry through the season.
