What We Measured
GYMGEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped. A direct measure of explosive leg power.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time. Rewards both height and speed of execution — the higher the score, the more explosive and efficient the movement.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump, divided by bodyweight. Shows how explosively force is generated per kilogram.
Leg Asymmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced; higher values indicate one leg is doing more work than the other.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force recorded during a maximal isometric pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). Rate of Force Development at 200 milliseconds during the Isometric Mid-Thigh Pull, expressed in Newtons per second (N/s). Captures how quickly maximal force is expressed in the first critical window — higher values indicate faster, more explosive neuromuscular function, which is directly relevant to acceleration and reactive performance in distance running.
Postural Sway
Sway Velocity. The average speed of the body's centre of pressure whilst standing still, in mm/s. Lower values indicate better postural stability and balance control.

Isaac, here's how you got on
What's working well. Your two strongest results are maximum force and leg asymmetry, and they tell a consistent story. An IMTP peak force of 2.93 ×BW puts you in the top 14% of competitive national runners — that is a genuine strength advantage, not just a relative one. Your leg asymmetry of 4.64% is well inside the 10% threshold where load imbalances start to cause problems, and it has held steady across both sessions, which means it's structural. Years of bilateral loading through Thai kickboxing have produced a body that pushes evenly off both legs — that translates directly into a cleaner, more repeatable stride and a lower injury risk profile. Your rate of force development at 3,600 N/s also sits in the top 28% of active males, which tells you that when you do produce force, you're starting to do it quickly. That's a meaningful improvement from the raw data and a positive direction of travel.
Where there's room to improve. Three metrics are sitting below where we'd want them. Reactive strength at 0.38 is the most significant — bottom 22% vs active males, bottom 13% vs competitive runners. This is the gap between having force and being able to use it elastically. Your tendons aren't currently being trained to store and return energy, which is exactly the mechanism that makes efficient distance running feel effortless over longer efforts. Jump height at 33.3 cm and explosive power at 29.3 W/kg are both in the average bracket, which isn't a problem in isolation, but they're connected to the same underlying quality: the ability to express force quickly under real movement conditions. Postural sway at 6.4 mm/s is mid-range — functional, but there's a meaningful stability gain available here that would support everything else.
What we're going to do about it. The training focus between now and your next session will target reactive strength directly. We'll introduce a short plyometric block — single-leg hops, pogos, and ankle stiffness drills — with an emphasis on minimising ground contact time rather than maximising height. This teaches your tendons to behave like springs rather than bystanders. Alongside that, single-leg stability work will address the postural sway score and reinforce the leg symmetry you've already earned. The strength base is there. The task now is to build the speed and elasticity on top of it. You should feel the difference on faster training runs before you see it on the next test.
