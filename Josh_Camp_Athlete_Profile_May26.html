What We Measured
ATHLEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped from a countermovement start. One of the most well-normed metrics in the report. Affected by technique, ankle mobility, and body proportions: taller athletes often show lower heights even with strong power output.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time. Rewards both how high and how quickly you jump: a measure of spring and explosiveness. Sensitive to technique and highly likely to improve with familiarity and ankle mobility work.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump per kilogram of bodyweight. Can be elevated by a deep countermovement; best interpreted alongside jump height and RSI together rather than in isolation.
Leg Symmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced. A well-normed and practically meaningful metric: one of the most reliable results in this report.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force produced during a maximal pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength with good literature support. Develops with physical maturity and structured loading: expect this to rise as you get older and stronger.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly force rises in the first 200 milliseconds of a maximal pull. Captures neuromuscular speed and explosiveness. Presented as a descriptive category rather than a ranked band in this report because it is highly sensitive to testing setup, sampling rate, and onset detection: it is most useful as a longitudinal tracking metric over time.
Postural Stability
Sway Velocity. Average speed of the body's centre of pressure while standing still. Lower is better. Presented as a descriptive category: most useful as a longitudinal baseline and for fatigue or return-to-play monitoring. Ankle mobility can directly affect this score, so improvement in dorsiflexion range is likely to move it.

A profile with a real standout and a clear direction

These results were recorded at the end of the season, which is worth keeping in mind when reading the numbers. Both the countermovement jump and the isometric pull are sensitive to accumulated fatigue: a long season of matches, training load, and physical wear tends to suppress both jump height and maximum force output. That does not change the direction of the profile, but it does mean the values for CMJ and IMTP in particular are likely a little conservative relative to what Josh is capable of fresh. The numbers here are still meaningful as a reference point; they just need to be read with that context in mind.

With that said, explosive power output is a genuine standout. At 40.6 W/kg you are in the top 10% of active men under 40, and that reading holds up in the upper third even against full-time professional footballers. There is a nuance worth understanding here: a deep countermovement during the jump can inflate peak power relative to what the jump height alone would suggest, and the combination of a high power figure alongside a 26.9 cm jump makes that worth noting. It still points to real fast-twitch muscle quality; it is just worth verifying at a pre-season retest with a more standardised approach. For a forward, that kind of power underpins first-step acceleration, physical contest at pace, and the ability to separate from opponents: it is an attribute that matters.

Your leg symmetry is also good: at 5.0% difference between left and right you are well inside the range considered balanced, in the upper third of active peers, and average against elite professional standards. Balanced force production between legs is associated with more even loading across the body over a long season and is a positive marker for robustness. It is a quality worth protecting.

What is developing

Jump height (26.9 cm) and reactive strength (RSI 0.33) are both below the active male under-40 average. As noted above, end-of-season fatigue is a likely contributor to both: CMJ height in particular tends to drop meaningfully when athletes are carrying accumulated load, and these values may understate what is achievable in a fresher state. That said, they are still areas to develop. RSI is also technique-sensitive and responds well to consistent practice of the movement; a pre-season retest will give a more accurate read on where things stand.

Maximum strength is the primary development target. At 2.57 times bodyweight the IMTP sits in the bottom third for active men under 40, and at the bottom 10% against professional players. Again, end-of-season fatigue genuinely suppresses isometric force output, so the true value in a recovered state is likely somewhat higher than recorded today; both attempts also had more baseline variability than ideal, which can further suppress the peak. Even accounting for both, there is a meaningful gap to close. For a forward, maximal lower body strength underpins the power output you can sustain, your capacity for physical contest, and your resilience over a full season. The gap between the current reading and the professional benchmark of 3.55 times bodyweight is closeable: the power quality already in the profile gives a strong foundation to build from across the off-season.

Postural stability sits in the average range for active men under 40, a solid baseline. Rate of force development is presented as descriptive only and is best treated as a longitudinal reference point, particularly given the baseline variability in today's IMTP attempts.
