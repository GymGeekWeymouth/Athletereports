End-of-Season Baseline: Session 1

This session was completed at the end of a long season with almost every minute played. End-of-season testing is valuable precisely because it captures a real-world snapshot: but it also means the numbers reflect accumulated fatigue rather than peak physical condition. Jump output and limb asymmetry in particular are sensitive to this. The most useful thing to do with today's results is to treat them as the baseline: the starting point that a pre-season retest will measure progress against. Force plate testing also has a technique component, and second-session numbers almost always improve simply from familiarity with the movements.

About the comparison data

Results are presented as banded classifications: Bottom 10%, Bottom Third, Average, Upper Third, Top 10%: rather than precise percentile ranks. This approach is more appropriate given the variability in normative datasets across equipment, protocols, and populations. Metrics with weaker normative foundations (RFD, postural sway) are presented as descriptive categories only. At 170 cm and 26 years old, Josh sits at the taller end of the youth population, which affects how some normalised metrics read: context is provided where relevant. All results are best interpreted alongside your coach in the context of your training history and goals.

What We Measured
ATHLEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped from a countermovement start. One of the most well-normed metrics in the report. Affected by technique, ankle mobility, and body proportions: taller athletes often show lower heights even with strong power output.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time. Rewards both how high and how quickly you jump: a measure of spring and explosiveness. Sensitive to technique and highly likely to improve with familiarity and ankle mobility work.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump per kilogram of bodyweight. Can be elevated by a deep countermovement; best interpreted alongside jump height and RSI together rather than in isolation.
Leg Symmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced. A well-normed and practically meaningful metric: one of the most reliable results in this report.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force produced during a maximal pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength with good literature support. Develops with physical maturity and structured loading: expect this to rise as you get older and stronger.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly force rises in the first 200 milliseconds of a maximal pull. Captures neuromuscular speed and explosiveness. Presented as a descriptive category rather than a ranked band in this report because it is highly sensitive to testing setup, sampling rate, and onset detection: it is most useful as a longitudinal tracking metric over time.
Postural Stability
Sway Velocity. Average speed of the body's centre of pressure while standing still. Lower is better. Presented as a descriptive category: most useful as a longitudinal baseline and for fatigue or return-to-play monitoring. Ankle mobility can directly affect this score, so improvement in dorsiflexion range is likely to move it.

Josh, here's how you got on
A strong start

First things first: this is your baseline, and first-time scores on force plates are almost always an underestimate of what someone can actually do. The testing has a real technique component to it: particularly the countermovement jump, where the depth, timing, and speed of the movement all affect the output. We see this consistently: athletes come back for their second session and post noticeably better numbers simply from being more comfortable with the equipment and the movements involved. So read today's results as a starting point, not a ceiling.

With that said, there is genuinely good news in this profile. Your leg symmetry is excellent: at 13.67% difference between left and right you are in the top 10% of active boys your age, and well-matched even against elite Under-18 academy standards. For a goalkeeper, that bilateral balance matters: clean footwork, consistent diving mechanics, and even load distribution across both legs across a long season all benefit from it. It is a quality that is hard to train directly and tends to reflect good general movement development: it is worth being pleased about.

Your explosive power output is also a positive: at 43.6 W/kg you are in the upper third of active peers and that reading holds up as strong even against Category 1 Under-18 academy players two years your senior. There are some nuances worth understanding here: a deep countermovement position during the jump can produce a higher peak power reading, and that may be part of what is happening with the combination of a high power figure and a 27 cm jump height. It is still a meaningful number and suggests real fast-twitch qualities, but it is best confirmed at your next session with a more standardised technique.

What is developing

Your jump height (27 cm) and reactive strength (RSI 0.42) are both below the active Under-16 average, and it is worth understanding why before drawing any strong conclusions. Three things are likely at play here, all of them addressable. The first is the first-test effect described above: these are the metrics most sensitive to technique and familiarity, so they will likely be among the biggest movers at your next session. The second is your height: at 170 cm you have considerably more body to get off the ground than most 16-year-olds, and the normative comparisons do not fully account for that: a tall goalkeeper jumping 27 cm is a different physical feat to a shorter peer doing the same. The third is your ankle mobility: restricted dorsiflexion limits how effectively force can be loaded and transferred through the lower leg during a jump, and this is directly trainable. Work on your ankle range is likely to produce a noticeable improvement in both of these metrics at your next test.

Your maximum force (2.21 times bodyweight) and rate of force development are both in the developing range, and this is simply the strength foundation catching up with your physical frame. At 26 and still developing physically, this is exactly the stage where structured loading starts to have the biggest effect: the gains available to a developing athlete in these metrics are larger and faster than at almost any other point in a career. There is a lot of room to move here, and that is an exciting position to be in.

Postural stability is flagged as elevated, which in the context of restricted ankle mobility is not surprising: the two are closely linked. This is best treated as a baseline to return to rather than a cause for concern: as ankle range improves, balance scores tend to follow.
