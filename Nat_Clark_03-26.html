What We Measured
GYMGEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped. A direct measure of explosive leg power.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time (eccentric + concentric + flight). Rewards both height and speed of execution. GymGeek uses the ForceDecks total-contraction-time method; values are typically lower than flight-time-only protocols.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump, divided by bodyweight. Shows how explosively force is generated per kilogram.
Leg Asymmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced; higher values indicate one leg is doing more work than the other.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force recorded during a maximal isometric pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly maximum force is expressed in the first 200 milliseconds of the pull, in Newtons per second. Higher values indicate faster, more explosive neuromuscular function. Calculated from raw CSV data using a 5-SD onset detection threshold against a 3-second baseline at 400 Hz.
Postural Sway
Sway Velocity. The average speed of the body's centre of pressure whilst standing still, in mm/s. Lower values indicate better postural stability and balance control.

Nat, here's how you got on
What's working well. The headline result from this session is your leg symmetry: 2.51% asymmetry puts you in the top 1% of actively training women over 40, and for someone working through a right hamstring tendinopathy that is a genuinely reassuring finding. The injury has not created a measurable loading imbalance between legs, at least as measured here. Your postural sway also came in at a reasonable level, placing you in the middle of the pack against your age group, which suggests your balance control is intact. And your maximum force production is a real standout: 2.29 times bodyweight on the isometric pull is strong for any active woman, placing you in the top 5% against your peers and top 17% against international-level female distance runners. A note here that IMTP scores are sensitive to testing setup and how hard you pull, so this is best read as a strong directional signal rather than a precise ranking; the direction is clearly positive.
Where there's room to improve. The jump-based metrics tell a different story. Jump height (12.2 cm), reactive strength (RSI 0.13), and explosive power (11.02 W/kg) all sit in the bottom few percent against active women your age. These three metrics are closely related: they all reflect how quickly and forcefully your legs can produce power during a fast, bouncy movement. Your rate of force development from the IMTP pull is also very low at 701 N/s. That result warrants a note: the force trace shows a gradual ramp rather than an explosive spike, which is a pattern consistent with a tentative or protective pull, the kind that can happen when a hamstring tendinopathy makes you cautious. It may not reflect your true explosive capacity, and a retest with explicit explosive intent cuing is recommended when symptoms allow. Your sway, while acceptable, has a little room to improve against the elite benchmark.
What to focus on next. The priority from this session is rebuilding the explosive side of your physical profile. Your strength base is already well above average: the next step is translating that into speed, spring, and quick-fire force production. As your tendinopathy rehab progresses and loading tolerance improves, activities that develop leg stiffness and reactive qualities, things like single-leg work, gentle bounding, and speed-based efforts in training, will start to close the gap between your strength numbers and your jump and power scores. Your asymmetry is excellent, so protecting that balance as you increase intensity is worth keeping in mind. A retest of your RFD with an explicitly explosive pull cue will also give a much cleaner read on your true neuromuscular speed once symptoms allow.
