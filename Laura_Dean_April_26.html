Unlike our general population benchmarks, there is no single published database covering all seven metrics for elite female cross-country skiers. The practical implication is that the percentile rankings on the right-hand cards are best read as a rough guide rather than a precise score: they tell you whether you are clearly above, roughly in line with, or clearly below elite level, but the exact number should not be taken too literally. The same general caution applies to all normative comparisons in this report: testing equipment, technique, and lab conditions all vary between studies, and those differences can move scores around. What We Measured
GYMGEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped. A direct measure of explosive leg power.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time (eccentric + concentric + flight). Rewards both height and speed of execution. GymGeek uses the ForceDecks total-contraction-time method; values are typically lower than flight-time-only protocols.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump, divided by bodyweight. Shows how explosively force is generated per kilogram.
Leg Asymmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced; higher values indicate one leg is doing more work than the other.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force recorded during a maximal isometric pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength. IMTP percentile scores are sensitive to testing setup and technique and should be read as directional indicators.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly maximum force is expressed in the first 200 milliseconds of the isometric pull. Higher values indicate faster, more explosive neuromuscular function. Values are sensitive to sampling frequency and onset method; GymGeek norms are calibrated to this specific protocol.
Postural Sway
Sway Velocity. The average speed of the body's centre of pressure whilst standing still, in mm/s. Lower values indicate better postural stability and balance control.

What's working well. The standout story from your results is the sheer quality of your lower body strength and neuromuscular power. Your leg symmetry is also acceptable at 8.64%, sitting just below the 10% clinical threshold. Postural sway at 6.9 mm/s is in the average range, which for skiing is a reasonable baseline for single-leg balance challenges on changing terrain.
Where there's room to improve. The clearest gap in your profile is in jump height and reactive strength, both sitting in the bottom 4% against active women your age. This contrast with your exceptional strength and RFD is striking: you have the engine, but the elastic, springy quality that allows a powerful athlete to bounce force quickly off the ground is currently underdeveloped. This is the physical quality most associated with dynamic skiing movement, particularly the quick leg-to-leg transitions and the ability to deal with terrain variation. Your leg asymmetry, while within clinical tolerance, is sitting at the lower end of the range and is worth tracking: any increase above 10% over time is a useful early signal worth noting.
The bigger picture. What this session tells us is that you are a physically capable athlete with a genuinely unusual profile. The strength and force production numbers are the kind of scores most people spend years trying to build, and you have them already. The jump and reactive strength scores are low not because your legs are weak, but because this particular quality, the fast, elastic bounce, tends to be trained quite differently to the grinding strength work that produces big pull and RFD numbers. It is also worth knowing that postural stability and leg symmetry are both in a reasonable place, which means the foundations are solid. The most interesting question your results raise is not whether you are fit, because clearly you are, but whether your physical preparation matches what skiing actually demands of you on the hill: quick reactions, fast weight transfers, and the ability to absorb and redirect force in a fraction of a second. That is where the opportunity sits.
