What We Measured
ATHLEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped from a countermovement start. One of the most well-normed metrics in the report. Affected by technique, lower-limb stiffness, and training background: endurance athletes often score lower here despite strong fitness.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time. Rewards both how high and how quickly you jump: a measure of spring and explosiveness. Sensitive to technique and highly likely to improve with familiarity.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump per kilogram of bodyweight. Can be elevated by a deep countermovement; best interpreted alongside jump height and RSI together rather than in isolation.
Leg Symmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced. A well-normed and practically meaningful metric: one of the most reliable results in this report.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force produced during a maximal pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength with good literature support. Develops with physical maturity and structured loading: expect this to rise as you get older and stronger.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly force rises in the first 200 milliseconds of a maximal pull. Captures neuromuscular speed and explosiveness. Presented as a descriptive category rather than a ranked band in this report because it is highly sensitive to testing setup, sampling rate, and onset detection: it is most useful as a longitudinal tracking metric over time.
Postural Stability
Sway Velocity. Average speed of the body's centre of pressure while standing still. Lower is better. Presented as a descriptive category: most useful as a longitudinal baseline and for fatigue or return-to-play monitoring. A steady score here is a good sign of well-controlled balance.

Abi, here's how you got on
A strong start

First things first: this is your baseline, taken right before the start of your tri season, and first-time scores on force plates are almost always an underestimate of what someone can actually do. The testing has a real technique component, particularly the countermovement jump, and athletes consistently post better numbers at their second session simply from familiarity with the equipment and the movements. So read today's results as a starting point, not a ceiling.

With that said, there is genuinely good news in this profile. Your leg symmetry is exceptional: at 0.65% difference between left and right you are about as balanced as force plate testing can measure, in the top 10% of competitive age-group triathletes and outstanding even against world-tour professionals. For a triathlete that bilateral balance matters: the run loads each leg thousands of times per session, and well-matched limbs spread that load evenly, supporting consistent mechanics and resilience across a long season. It is a quality worth being genuinely pleased about, and worth protecting as your race load builds.

Your relative strength is another real positive: a maximum pull of 2.12 times bodyweight puts you in the upper third of competitive age-group triathletes and sits right at the average for full-time world-tour professionals. Heading into the season with that foundation already in place is a strong position to be in.

Your explosive power reading was very high at 30.0 W/kg, landing in the top 10% against both comparison groups. One honest nuance here: a deep countermovement position during the jump can inflate the peak power reading, and the combination of a very high power figure with an 18.5 cm jump height suggests that may be part of what happened today. It is still a meaningful signal, but it is best confirmed at your next session with a more standardised jump technique.

What is developing

Your jump height (18.5 cm) and reactive strength (RSI 0.21) are both below the age-group average, and it is worth understanding why before drawing any strong conclusions. This is the classic endurance athlete profile: triathlon training builds a deep aerobic engine and a solid strength base, but it gives the fast, elastic qualities measured by these tests far less practice. The scores reflect training emphasis, not a limitation. They are also the two most technique-sensitive tests on the plates, so the first-test learning effect applies most strongly here: expect both to be among the biggest movers at retest. These qualities also tend to respond quickly in endurance athletes once given attention, with knock-on benefits for run economy.

Your rate of force development (3283 N/s) is recorded as a descriptive baseline only. The baseline period in today's pulls carried a little noise, which makes this number most useful as a longitudinal reference to track over the season rather than a ranked score.

Postural stability came in steady at 4.8 mm/s: a good, settled baseline. Over a season this is one of the more useful numbers to return to, as balance scores can drift when fatigue accumulates, making it a quiet but practical readiness marker.
