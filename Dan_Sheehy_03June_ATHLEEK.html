This is your baseline: Session 1

Force plate testing has a real learning curve. The countermovement jump and isometric pull both have a technique component, and first-time scores frequently sit slightly below what someone is genuinely capable of. That is entirely normal and one of the most consistent findings we see: athletes almost always improve on their second test simply from being more comfortable with the equipment and the movement demands. Think of today as your starting point: a concrete snapshot to build on and measure progress against.

About the comparison data

Results are presented as banded classifications: Bottom 10%, Bottom Third, Average, Upper Third, Top 10%: rather than precise percentile ranks. This approach is more appropriate given the variability in normative datasets across equipment, protocols, and populations. Metrics with weaker normative foundations (RFD, postural sway) are presented as descriptive categories only. Note: all three IMTP baseline windows showed slightly elevated noise (b_SD 12.8–13.3 N); attempt 1 was selected as best valid (highest RFD). All results are best interpreted alongside your coach in the context of your training history and goals.

What We Measured
ATHLEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped from a countermovement start. One of the most well-normed metrics in the report. Sensitive to technique, countermovement depth, and hip mobility: all of which are addressable with practice.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time. A measure of spring and explosiveness: it rewards both how high and how quickly you jump. Your RSI is exceptional because you moved very fast through the countermovement, which the metric captures directly.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump per kilogram of bodyweight. Your reading of 49.2 W/kg is exceptional. Can be elevated by a deep countermovement; best interpreted alongside RSI and jump height together rather than in isolation.
Leg Symmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced. Your 6.2% indicates no meaningful imbalance.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force produced during a maximal pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength. Responds directly to structured strength loading: one of the most trainable metrics in this profile.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly force rises in the first 200 milliseconds of a maximal pull. Presented as descriptive only because it is highly sensitive to baseline noise, sampling rate, and onset detection: all three attempts today had slightly elevated baseline variation. Most valuable as a longitudinal tracking metric once test conditions are tightened.
Postural Stability
Sway Velocity. Average speed of the body's centre of pressure while standing still. Lower is better. Your 4.5 mm/s indicates excellent postural control. Presented descriptive only: most useful as a longitudinal baseline and for monitoring fatigue and readiness across a season.

Dan, here's how you got on
A profile with genuine elite qualities

The headline from today is straightforward: your power and reactive strength numbers are exceptional. At 49.2 W/kg, your peak power output sits comfortably above the average for active men under 40 and above the average for Premier League and EFL professionals. That is not a common result: most people who come through here, regardless of sport or background, do not produce numbers in that range. Your RSI of 0.69 tells the same story from a different angle: that figure puts you in the top 10% of active men under 40, and in the upper third even against full-time professional footballers. Together, these two results paint a picture of a player with an exceptional fast-twitch engine: quick, spring-loaded, and naturally explosive in the kind of short-burst movement that matters for a wide player.

It is worth being honest about one nuance here. Peak power in the countermovement jump is influenced by how deeply and quickly you dip before jumping, and a fast or deep countermovement can contribute to a higher reading. That does not invalidate the result, but it does mean the number is best confirmed at your next session with a standardised technique so we can have full confidence in it. RSI is also somewhat technique-sensitive. The directional conclusion remains the same either way: your fast-twitch qualities are a clear asset.

What is developing

The flip side of the profile is maximum force. Your IMTP came in at 2.67 times bodyweight, which is in the lower range versus both the active male population and the professional benchmark. There is an interesting pattern here: you have elite-level power production but a developing strength ceiling, which means your muscles are expressing a very high proportion of what they currently have available, but there is a meaningful amount of additional output sitting on the table once the force base grows. For a winger, maximum strength has a direct downstream effect on acceleration, contact situations, and resilience across a physical season: it is the quality that tends to be most responsive to structured loading in the gym.

Jump height at 28.3 cm is similarly below average versus peers. The two are connected: as your IMTP improves, jump height tends to follow, particularly with some plyometric work layered in. It is also worth flagging that today's IMTP baseline windows showed slightly elevated movement noise across all three attempts, which can affect the accuracy of the force trace and the RFD calculation. A slightly quieter baseline period before each pull at your next test would give us cleaner data: this is a setup note rather than anything to do with your effort or execution.

Your leg symmetry at 6.2% is in the well-balanced range: no meaningful left-right imbalance. Your postural stability (4.5 mm/s sway velocity) is very low, indicating excellent balance and postural control: a genuine positive that sits alongside the power numbers as a marker of good underlying movement quality.
