What We Measured
GYMGEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped. A direct measure of explosive leg power.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time. Rewards both height and speed of execution. GymGeek uses the total-contraction-time method.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump, divided by bodyweight.
Leg Asymmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force during a maximal isometric pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. IMTP scores are sensitive to testing setup and technique; the percentile is best read as a strong directional indicator.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly maximal force is expressed in the first 200 milliseconds of the pull. Calculated using 5-SD onset detection against a 3-second baseline at 400 Hz.
Postural Sway
Sway Velocity. The average speed of the body's centre of pressure whilst standing still. Lower values indicate better postural stability.

Paul, here's how you got on
What's working well. The headline from today is your neuromuscular quality: your Rate of Force Development came in at 3937 N/s, which is top 1% for active men your age and more than double the group average. That is genuinely exceptional. It tells us your nervous system fires quickly and cleanly, and is exactly the kind of quality that protects health and function as the years go on. Your Maximum Force is strong too, at 2.25 times bodyweight: top 10% of the over-60 group and solidly above average even against men a decade younger. Given the hip history, it is very encouraging to see the lower body producing force at that level and doing so equally from both sides. Your Leg Asymmetry was effectively zero during the jump: a rare and pleasing result. Postural Sway is top 7% for your age group, and Reactive Strength sits comfortably in the top quarter. On most of the metrics that matter for long-term resilience and physical capability, you are well ahead of the curve.
Where there's room to improve. Jump Height is the one number that stands apart from the rest: 12.9 cm puts you in the bottom 7% of active over-60 males, and it sits in striking contrast to your strength and power scores. You have all the ingredients for a better jump: the force, the speed of force production, and the balance. Something in the translation from force to flight is holding the number back, whether that is technique, the movement pattern itself, or the way the hip history has shaped how you approach the movement. Reactive Strength, whilst good against your own peer group, sits at the 48th percentile compared to men a decade younger: there is headroom there to close.
