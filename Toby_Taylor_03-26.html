What We Measured
GYMGEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped. A direct measure of explosive leg power.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time (eccentric + concentric + flight). Rewards both height and speed of execution. GymGeek uses the ForceDecks total-contraction-time method; values are typically lower than flight-time-only protocols.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump, divided by bodyweight. Shows how explosively force is generated per kilogram.
Leg Asymmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced; higher values indicate one leg is doing more work than the other.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force recorded during a maximal isometric pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly force builds in the first 200 milliseconds of the isometric pull, in Newtons per second (N/s). A high RFD means your nervous system fires fast, which translates directly to explosiveness on the pitch. Calculated using a 5-SD onset detection threshold against a 3-second baseline.
Postural Sway
Sway Velocity. The average speed of the body's centre of pressure whilst standing still, in mm/s. Lower values indicate better postural stability and balance control.

What's working well

The headline result is your Maximum Force: 3.00 times your bodyweight in the isometric pull, putting you in the top 8% of active boys your age and matching or exceeding a typical PL/EFL Academy U16 player on this measure. That is a serious number, and it tells us the foundational strength is genuinely there. Your Jump Height (33.3 cm, 75th percentile vs active U16 males) is also solid; you're clearing most of your peers in the air. Given that you've been through a double leg fracture to the right, these strength and jump numbers are particularly encouraging: the rehabilitation has clearly done its job in terms of raw force production. Your Leg Asymmetry at 6.33% is also a pleasing result. Both legs are contributing fairly evenly to the jump, which after that kind of injury history reflects well on where you've got to.
Where there's room to improve

The gap between your raw strength and your explosive output is the defining feature of this profile. Despite having excellent force capacity, your Explosive Power (22.9 W/kg, 15th percentile) and Reactive Strength (RSI 0.34, 31st percentile) sit low against active peers and considerably lower against academy-level comparison. Your legs can generate serious force, but you're not yet converting it quickly into movement. Rate of Force Development at 2640 N/s is in the average band against active boys but sits in the bottom 4% against academy players, reinforcing the same picture: fast force expression is the area that needs to develop. Think of it as having the engine, but the gearbox still needs tuning.

Postural Sway (9.4 mm/s, bottom 5%) is the most clinically significant finding in this report. This is very likely a direct consequence of the right leg fracture and the disrupted balance feedback that follows long bone injuries. It needs deliberate targeted work and will not resolve with general training alone.
