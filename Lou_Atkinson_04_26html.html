What We Measured
GYMGEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped. A direct measure of explosive leg power.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time (eccentric + concentric + flight). Rewards both height and speed of execution. GymGeek uses the ForceDecks total-contraction-time method; values are typically lower than flight-time-only protocols.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump, divided by bodyweight. Shows how explosively force is generated per kilogram.
Leg Asymmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced; higher values indicate one leg is doing more work.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force recorded during a maximal isometric pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength. Note: IMTP scores are sensitive to testing setup and technique and should be read as a directional indicator rather than a precise ranking.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). How quickly maximum force is expressed in the first 200 milliseconds of the pull. Higher values indicate faster, more explosive neuromuscular function. Calculated using a 5-SD onset detection threshold. Values are sensitive to sampling frequency and onset method; GymGeek norms are calibrated to this specific protocol.
Postural Sway
Sway Velocity. The average speed of the body's centre of pressure whilst standing still, in mm/s. Lower values indicate better postural stability and balance control.

Lou, here's how you got on
What's working well. The headline from this session is that your power and strength numbers are genuinely outstanding. Your explosive power (26.3 W/kg) and reactive strength (RSI 0.39) both land at the very top of the active-over-50 female population, and your maximum isometric force at 2.36 times bodyweight puts you above 99% of women in your age group on that measure. Bear in mind that IMTP scores are sensitive to testing setup and technique, so that ranking is best read as a strong directional indicator rather than a precise figure; but taken together with the power numbers, the picture is very consistent. Your leg symmetry is also excellent: just 2.5% difference between left and right is well within the healthy range and reflects the balanced, bilateral nature of cycling and yoga practice. Postural sway at 5.2 mm/s is similarly impressive, sitting in the top 7% of your age group. As a yoga instructor you will not be surprised by the balance result, but it is worth noting that very few people combine that level of stability with the kind of raw strength and power you are showing here.
Where there's room to improve. The one metric that stands out as notably lower is jump height at 13.3 cm, which sits at the 21st percentile of active women over 50. This is the most interesting finding in the report because it sits in clear contrast to the strength and power data. The countermovement jump requires a different kind of coordination to cycling: a fast, reflexive whole-body drive that depends on stretch-shortening cycle efficiency. Your rate of force development is solid at 1,871 N/s (75th percentile of active over-50 women), but against elite triathletes it falls to the 1st percentile, which tells you that the speed of force expression under very high competitive demand is an area with real headroom. These two metrics, jump height and RFD, are closely related: developing the ability to express force more rapidly would likely lift both.
The bigger picture. What this session tells us is that you have an unusual combination of qualities that most people never develop together. Strong, symmetrical, stable and powerful: those four things sitting alongside each other in the same profile is genuinely rare, and it is a clear reflection of a training background built on movement quality as much as output. The gap between your jump height and the rest of your scores is not a weakness in the conventional sense; it is simply a quality that cycling and yoga do not train, and it is the most interesting finding from this test because it tells us there is something specific to work on against an otherwise excellent baseline. The elite triathlon comparison puts that in perspective too: your strength, power, and symmetry hold up well against World Tour athletes; the jump and RFD gap is where the ceiling of that level is most visible. That is exactly the kind of information worth having before deciding where to focus next.
