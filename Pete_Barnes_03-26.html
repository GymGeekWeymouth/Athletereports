A note on benchmarking for hip replacement
We looked into whether published normative data exists for force plate testing — CMJ, IMTP, and RFD — in hip replacement patients specifically. The honest answer is that it does not. The research on total hip arthroplasty outcomes overwhelmingly uses functional tests such as timed up-and-go, sit-to-stand, and 6-minute walk rather than force plates, and no peer-reviewed normative dataset for this population on force plate metrics has been published to date. Rather than compare you to a group with no valid reference data, we have used active men over 60 as the second comparison group. Hip replacements are most prevalent in adults in their 60s and 70s, making this the closest meaningful age-matched benchmark available. It is an imperfect proxy, but it is the most honest one.

What We Measured
GYMGEEKAnalyseMetric Glossary
What the numbers mean
A plain-language guide to every metric in this report
Jump Height
CMJ Height. How high off the ground you jumped. A direct measure of explosive leg power.
Reactive Strength
RSI Modified. Jump height divided by total movement time (eccentric + concentric + flight). Rewards both height and speed of execution — the higher the score, the more explosive and efficient the movement. GymGeek uses the ForceDecks total-contraction-time method; values are typically lower than flight-time-only protocols.
Explosive Power
Peak Power (rel.). The highest power output during the jump, divided by bodyweight. Shows how explosively force is generated per kilogram.
Leg Asymmetry
Limb Asymmetry (CMJ). The percentage difference in force between left and right leg during the jump. Under 10% is considered well-balanced; higher values indicate one leg is doing more work than the other.
Maximum Force
IMTP Peak Force. The maximum force recorded during a maximal isometric pull against a fixed bar, relative to bodyweight. A measure of overall lower body strength.
Rate of Force Development
RFD @ 200ms (IMTP). Rate of Force Development at 200 milliseconds during the Isometric Mid-Thigh Pull, expressed in Newtons per second (N/s). Captures how quickly maximal force is expressed in the first critical window — higher values indicate faster, more explosive neuromuscular function. Calculated using a 5-SD onset detection threshold against a 3-second baseline. Values are sensitive to sampling frequency and onset method; GymGeek norms are calibrated to this specific protocol.
Postural Sway
Sway Velocity. The average speed of the body's centre of pressure whilst standing still, in mm/s. Lower values indicate better postural stability and balance control.

Pete, here's how you got on
What's working well Your Maximum Force result stands out as the highlight of this session. At 3.03 times your bodyweight on the isometric pull, you are producing lower body force at a level that puts you well into the top percentile for men over 50. That is a significant result, particularly given your hip replacement, and suggests the structural strength around the joint has held up well. Your leg symmetry is also solid at 8.56%, sitting comfortably in the average range; both legs are sharing the load at a level where we would not raise a flag. Postural Sway came in at 8.0 mm/s, which lands in the average-to-good zone for your age group; your balance and stability control are in decent shape.
Where there is room to improve The clearest gap in this session is between your strength and your ability to express it quickly. Jump Height at 23.3 cm, Reactive Strength at 0.17, and Explosive Power at 16.6 W/kg all sit in the lower range for men over 50. These metrics are closely connected: they all depend on the nervous system driving the muscles fast as well as hard. After a hip replacement, the brain naturally protects the joint by dampening that explosive output, and what you are seeing in these numbers is that process still playing out. Rate of Force Development came in at 2,216 N/s, which puts you in the average range for men over 50 and into the top third for men over 60. That is an encouraging result given the context of your hip replacement: your nervous system is driving force early in the movement at a level that holds up well against your age peers.
What to focus on next The priority from this session is rebuilding the speed side of your physical profile. Your strength is clearly there; the next step is teaching the nervous system to use it fast again. Activities that involve quick, confident leg movements: things like gentle plyometric work, stair climbing with intent, brisk walking and short hill efforts, will gradually close the gap between your strength score and your reactive and explosive scores. Symmetry is already solid, so maintaining that as you push the intensity is important. Postural Sway is decent but always worth keeping an eye on: single-leg balance and strength work is a low-effort way to keep that moving in the right direction.
